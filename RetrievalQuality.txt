This dependence has driven the adoption of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architectures, which combine external knowledge retrieval with generative models to enhance factual grounding and reasoning in applications such as question answering systems, academic assistants, and conversational agents. Recent research shows that many LLM limitations arise from poor retrieval rather than weak generation, as low relevance, noisy or misleading documents, weak ranking, and excessive context length reduce reasoning accuracy. Even advanced models like GPT-4, Claude, and Llama-3 exhibit performance degradation under poorly curated contexts, with reported accuracy drops of 14–85% as context length increases. Moreover, retrieval quality has complex effects on model behavior: while misleading information increases hallucinations, limited inclusion of weakly relevant content can improve robustness. This study analyzes how retrieval relevance, noise, and context length affect reasoning accuracy and hallucinations in RAG systems, showing that boosting-based models, particularly XGBoost, achieve the highest accuracy, emphasizing the importance of optimized retrieval strategies..1. IntroductionLarge Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as a transformative force in artificial intelligence, enabling machines to process, understand, and generate human-like language at an unprecedented scale. Models such as GPT-4, Claude, and Llama-3 have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of natural language processing (NLP) tasks, including question answering, text summarization, code generation, academic writing, and conversational dialogue. These advancements have led to the rapid integration of LLMs into real-world systems spanning education, healthcare, finance, research assistance, and enterprise automation.Despite their impressive generative abilities, LLMs exhibit a fundamental limitation: they do not inherently possess up-to-date or domain-specific knowledge beyond their training data. Moreover, LLMs are prone to producing hallucinated or factually incorrect responses when required to reason over incomplete, ambiguous, or misleading information. To address these shortcomings, Retrieval- Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a dominant architectural paradigm. RAG systems enhance LLMs by retrieving relevant information from external knowledge sources—such as document repositories, databases, or the web—and incorporating this retrieved context into the model’s input during generation.BackgroundThe RAG framework operates under the assumption that providing relevant and factual external context enables LLMs to generate more accurate, grounded, and explainable outputs. Early research in information retrieval and neural generation demonstrated that access to curated documents significantly improves factual consistency and reduces hallucinations. As a result, modern RAG pipelines typically consist of three main components: (1) a retriever that selects candidate documents, (2) a ranking or filtering mechanism to refine the retrieved content, and (3) a generative model that synthesizes a response based on the provided context.However, recent empirical evidence suggests that the effectiveness of RAG systems is highly sensitive to retrieval quality. Retrieval errors—such as low relevance, poor ranking, excessive redundancy, or the inclusion of noisy or distracting documents—can significantly degrade LLM performance. Even when retrieval succeeds in returning relevant documents, excessively long contexts may overwhelm the model’s attention mechanisms, leading to reasoning failures and actual inaccuracies. Studies have shown that LLMs struggle to identify the most salient information when faced with long or cluttered contexts, resulting in degraded answer quality.Notably, Du et al. (EMNLP 2025) demonstrated that increasing input context length can lead to performance drops ranging from 14% to 85%, even when relevant information is present. This finding challenges the common assumption that “more context is always better” and highlights the importance of careful context selection and management. Additionally, other studies reveal counterintuitive behaviors, such as improved robustness when small amounts of random or weakly relevant information are introduced, indicating that LLMs exhibit non-linear responses to retrieval noise.MotivationThe growing reliance on RAG-based systems in high-stakes applications makes retrieval quality a critical concern. In domains such as medical decision support, legal research, academic reasoning, and enterprise knowledge management, inaccurate or hallucinated responses can lead to serious consequences. While substantial research effort has been dedicated to improving LLM architectures and scaling model size, comparatively less attention has been given to systematically analyzing how retrieval quality affects downstream model performance.This imbalance in research focus motivates the need to shift attention from model-centric improvements to retrieval-centric evaluation and optimization. Understanding how factors such as relevance precision, noise tolerance, document ranking, and context length influence LLM reasoning and factual accuracy is essential for building reliable and trustworthy systems. Furthermore, existing evaluation benchmarks often conflate retrieval failure with reasoning failure, making it difficult to isolate the true causes of performance degradation.Therefore, this research is motivated by the need to provide a comprehensive and structured analysis of retrieval quality within RAG systems. By synthesizing recent findings and examining the nuanced interactions between retrieval strategies and generative reasoning, this work aims to identify best practices for context construction and retrieval optimization. Ultimately, the goal is to inform the design of more robust, accurate, and efficient LLM-based systems that can be safely deployed in real-world, knowledge-intensive applications.Literature ReviewThis section synthesizes current research regarding how deep learning architectures manage context and how machine learning metrics are evolving to measure the specific "quality" of retrieved information.Deep Learning Architectures and Context ManagementRecent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) indicate that model performance is not solely governed by parameter scale or training data, but is increasingly constrained by architectural efficiency and context management strategies. As LLMs are deployed in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems, they are frequently required to process large volumes of retrieved information. However, emerging research demonstrates that excessive or poorly structured context can significantly degrade reasoning and factual accuracy.A pivotal study by Du et al. (EMNLP 2025) revealed that increasing input context length alone can severely harm LLM performance, with accuracy drops ranging from 14% to 85% across various reasoning and question-answering tasks. Importantly, this degradation was observed even when relevant information was present within the context, suggesting that failures arise from context overload rather than retrieval failure. This finding challenges the widely held assumption that providing more retrieved documents inherently improves LLM outputs and highlights the limitations of transformer attention mechanisms when dealing with long, cluttered inputs.To address these limitations, several architectural innovations have been proposed to improve context handling efficiency. Xiao et al. (2024) introduced DuoAttention, a novel attention mechanism that separates attention heads into two functional groups: Retrieval Heads, which capture global contextual dependencies, and Streaming Heads, which focus on localized, sequential information. This separation allows the model to allocate computational resources more effectively, reducing memory usage by 2.55× while maintaining high accuracy. DuoAttention enables LLMs to process multi-million-token contexts, making it particularly suitable for long- document reasoning and large-scale RAG pipelines.Beyond internal architectural enhancements, researchers have proposed novel RAG system designs that actively involve LLMs in the retrieval process itself. Li et al. (2023) introducedLLatrieval, an LLM-verified retrieval framework that incorporates a verify–update loop. In this approach, the LLM evaluates the sufficiency of retrieved information and iteratively triggers additional retrieval steps when knowledge gaps are detected. This dynamic retrieval strategy improved answer quality by 3.4%, demonstrating that LLM-guided retrieval refinement can enhance factual grounding and completeness.Similarly, Tang et al. (2024) proposed Self-Retrieval, an end-to-end framework in which a single LLM performs both indexing and generation tasks. By eliminating the need for traditional dense or sparse retrievers, Self-Retrieval leverages the internal knowledge representation of the LLM to perform retrieval-like operations. Experimental results showed that this unified model outperformed conventional retrieval pipelines in both efficiency and answer accuracy, suggesting a promising direction toward fully integrated RAG architectures.Further extending this unification paradigm, Zhang et al. introduced LLM-Embedder, a single embedding model designed to support diverse retrieval tasks, including semantic search, ranking, and classification. Their approach achieved substantial improvements on the MMLU benchmark, reaching 49% performance, and demonstrated that a unified embedding space can effectively bridge retrieval and reasoning objectives.For complex, multi-hop reasoning tasks, Xu et al. (2025) proposed Collab-RAG, a multi-agent RAG framework in which specialized LLM agents collaborate to solve queries. In this system, a specialist agent decomposes complex questions into sub-tasks, which are then addressed by an Expert agent with targeted retrieval. This cooperative strategy improved reasoning accuracy by up to 14.2%, highlighting the benefits of task decomposition and agent specialization in retrieval- heavy reasoning scenarios.In parallel, efforts have been made to improve system efficiency and latency. A Modular RAG approach, building on the work of Ma et al. (2023), introduced conditional retrieval mechanisms such as query rewriting, knowledge filtering, and retrieval triggering. By invoking retrieval only when necessary, this architecture reduced response latency by 46% while preserving answer quality. These findings emphasize that intelligent retrieval control is essential for scalable, real- time LLM applications.Machine Learning Metrics, Evaluation, and Quality ControlWhile architectural innovations address how LLMs process retrieved information, a parallel body of research focuses on how retrieval quality is defined, measured, and optimized. Traditional information retrieval metrics such as recall, precision, and ranking quality remain fundamental indicators of RAG system performance.Alinejad, Kumar, and Vahdat (2024) conducted a comprehensive evaluation of retrieval components in LLM-based question-answering systems. Their study demonstrated a strong correlation between retrieval recall and downstream QA accuracy, showing that increasing retrieval depth from Recall@1 to Recall@10 improved answer accuracy from 0.666 to 0.796. These results confirm that retrieval effectiveness directly influences LLM performance, particularly in fact-intensive tasks.However, subsequent research reveals that retrieval quality cannot be defined solely by relevance metrics. Cuconasu, Trappolini, and Silvestri (2024), in their study “The Power of Noise: Redefining Retrieval for RAG Systems,” uncovered a nuanced relationship between noise and performance. While distracting documents—those closely related but misleading—degraded performance by up to 25%, the inclusion of random, clearly irrelevant documents unexpectedly improved accuracy by 35% in certain tasks. This counterintuitive finding suggests that LLMs may benefit from contrastive signals or noise that helps distinguish relevant information, underscoring the complexity of context composition.Beyond classical metrics, LLMs themselves are increasingly employed as evaluation and quality control agents. Thomas et al. (2024) demonstrated that LLMs can accurately predict searcher preferences and query performance, matching or even exceeding the reliability of human evaluators. This capability enables rapid, scalable evaluation of retrieval systems without the need for extensive manual labeling.Building on this idea, Meng et al. (2025) proposed using LLM-generated relevance judgments for Query Performance Prediction (QPP), significantly accelerating evaluation cycles in retrieval systems. While these approaches offer substantial efficiency gains, they also introduce new risks.The Cocktail Benchmark (2024) exposed a critical alignment issue known as source bias, where neural retrievers disproportionately favor LLM-generated text over human-written content. Thisbias can distort retrieval outcomes, amplify synthetic content, and reduce the diversity and reliability of retrieved knowledge, posing long-term risks to information ecosystems.To mitigate such alignment challenges, Jin et al. (2024) proposed LARPO (LLM Alignment as Retriever Optimization), reframing alignment as a retriever optimization problem. By using reward models as re-rankers, LARPO aligns retrieval outputs with the generator’s preferences and reasoning patterns. The selection is guided by the need for real-world relevance, scalability, and compatibility with modern retrieval architectures.Dataset SelectionNatural Questions (NQ-Open)Natural Questions (NQ) is a gold-standard benchmark in open-domain question answering. Unlike synthetic datasets, it consists of real anonymized queries issued to the Google search engine by actual users.Scale and Scope: The "Open" version of the dataset removes the specific document pointers, forcing the model to search through a massive corpus of ~21 million Wikipedia passages.Why it's used for Retrieval Quality:Real-world Ambiguity: Because the questions are organic, they often containlinguistic nuances or ambiguity that test a retriever’s semantic understanding.Information Density: With 21 million chunks, it provides a high-probability environment for "distractors"—passages that contain keywords from the query but do not actually contain the answer.Benchmarking Noise: It allows researchers to inject controlled amounts of "random noise" (unrelated Wikipedia pages) versus "harmful noise" (related but incorrect pages) to measure LLM tolerance.TechniquesThe research utilizes a sophisticated pipeline that moves from basic retrieval to advanced alignment.Embedding Models (The Retrievers)We utilize Dense Retrieval models, which convert text into high-dimensional vectors to capture semantic meaning rather than just matching keywords.E5-large-v2: A leading model from Microsoft that uses a "query-passage" prefixing strategy. This is used to test the LLM's "attention robustness"—its ability to ignore clutter to find the "gold" information.Retriever–Generator Alignment (LARPO)Standard RAG systems often suffer from a "mismatch": the retriever picks documents based on similarity, but the LLM might prefer documents that are easier to reason with.LARPO (LLM Alignment as Retriever Optimization): This strategy treats the retriever like a student being coached by the LLM. It uses Preference Optimization (similar to RLHF) to train the retriever to prioritize documents that the LLM successfully uses to answer questions correctly. Using the Natural Questions dataset, hallucinated and correct answers were simulated to evaluate how context relevance, noise, and length influence LLM reasoning. Multiple machine learning models— Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost, Gradient Boosting, and MLP Neural Network— were applied to detect hallucinations, with results analyzed through accuracy, log loss, and efficiency metrics. Graphical analysis highlighted model performance differences, showing that boosting-based models like XGBoost and Gradient Boosting achieved the highest accuracy and reliability, while Logistic Regression provided the fastest and most efficient predictions.
